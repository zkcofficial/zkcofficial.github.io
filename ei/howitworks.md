## Terms used

First off, to be able to understand the structure of the algorithim, we have to understand some of the terms which will be discussed here. So, each installment of Ethnographic Odessy is called by a specific name - with the first being called <i>Genesis</i>.<br>
<i>Genesis</i>, in addition to all the other installments of Ethnographic Odessey, are split into four sections called <i>Cycles</i>.<br>
Each <i>Cycle</i> is further divided into three <i>Parts</i>. The basic machine I use for this algorithim is called <i>The Box</i>.
<hr>

## <i>The Box</i><br>

The box is a box (who could have guessed) with four compartments. Each compartment represents a <i>theme</i>, with each theme acting as a reminder for the person 
creating the story (me in this case) to follow that general theme when creating the story. The way this lore algorithim basically works is it is divided into <i>turns</i>,
with each turn corresponding to one of the three <i>Parts</i> (as discussed above). Each <i>Part</i>, and thus compartment/theme represents 70 in-game years, with all three <i>Parts</i>, which is one 
<i>Cycle</i> representing 210 years all together. After one turn, the story progresses into the next <i>Part</i>, corresponding to the next compartment, which has a 
different theme. <br>

Now, you may have noticed something interesting if you were paying close attention: Each <i>Cycle</i> only adds up to three <i>Parts</i>, meaning if each Part corresponds 
to one compartment in <i>The Box</i>, which has four different compartments each representing a unique theme, then each Cycle only covers three out of the four 
themes. This is on purpose, as I have found that if each of the four Cycles has three Parts which rotate through a set of four Themes, then only on the beginning of the 
fifth rotation does the cycle of the <i>The Box</i> restart. <br>

This is very helpful, as it allows me to accuratly and easily space each installment of Ethnographic Odessey. What do I mean?
Each installment will include a minimum of four <i>Cycles</i>; which is helpful because each of the four <i>Cycles</i> has an average word count. You can decide your average word count on a personal basis,
but by knowing my average word count and having a structured method for creating content, I can work with an easy structure which allows me to create content reliably and on a schedule.
<hr>

How does this affect the game, I hear you ask? Well it's simple. WIth the entering of each <i>Part</i>, the beginning of the next 70 years start. This means
a new theme is started which influences the way events are planned. What do I mean by planned? I mean if you get into the mind of each individual ruler - king, queen etc., then 
you can decide their descisions in relation to each other based on the theme of that <i>Part</i>. So if, for example, the current theme is 'War', then you can decide/make up 
justifications for the ruler(s) to declare wars on each other. You then War-game out the reactions from the parties the ruler(s) declared war on, going back and forth 
from the mind of ruler(s) to ruler(s) in deciding and executing actions until a stable situation has been reached between the parties involved, writing in a cinematic 
style all of the drama which occurs. If I have war-gamed out a conflict or generally good story involving some kingdoms but not others in the world, then I will usually 
just repeat the process for the untouched kingdoms. After all of this, I decide how many children each ruler has and I create and record their name using an etamology system I have previously 
recorded here, and I decide who inherits/takes control of each realm/governmental body. Using this system, in addition to the etamological system I have developed, I can create 
in-game culture, references and history, thus creating a rich tapestry of culture based on region and ethnicity. All of it is entirely fictional and repeatable, 
which I am incredibly proud of! <br>
